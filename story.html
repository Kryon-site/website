Founder's letter

Why I built Kryon.

A founder's letter.

The problem I kept seeing

For years before building Kryon, I worked as an operations consultant. I spent time inside dozens of businesses — from 10-person startups to 200-person scale-ups — helping them figure out why things weren't moving as fast as they should.

The answer was almost always the same: repetitive work. Not complex strategy problems. Not product decisions. Just the same tasks, done by hand, over and over, every single day. A spreadsheet copied into a CRM. An invoice checked and forwarded. A new customer account created in six different systems one by one.

These weren't small problems. I watched talented people spend 40% of their week doing things that didn't require any of their talent at all. I watched operations teams burn out trying to keep up. I watched founders accept that "this is just how it is" — that a certain baseline of manual drudgery was the cost of running a business.

I didn't accept that. And I started looking for tools that could help.

What I tried first

The automation tools that existed fell into two camps. The consumer tools were simple enough to use but broke constantly, couldn't handle anything real, and fell apart the moment a workflow needed more than two or three steps. The enterprise tools were powerful but required a developer to set up, a developer to maintain, and a budget most of the businesses I worked with didn't have.

There was a giant gap in the middle. Businesses that were too sophisticated for the toy tools, and too lean to hire engineers just to run their internal operations.

I tried patching together combinations of these tools. Zapier here, a bit of Python there, a webhook pointed at this API. It worked, kind of. Then it broke. Then it needed someone to fix it. Then the person who understood how it worked left, and it all came undone.

I kept thinking: this should be easier. Not just simpler — actually easier. For anyone. Without a developer on call.

"Automation should be accessible to any business, not just the ones that can afford to hire engineers to build it."
K
Founder & CEO, Kryon Written March 2026

What I built

I spent 18 months building Kryon. Not as a side project. As the thing I woke up at 5am to work on, the thing I was still at my desk thinking about at midnight.

The core insight was this: the hard part of automation isn't the technical connection between systems. APIs exist for that. The hard part is the reasoning — what should happen when an exception occurs, how should context carry through a multi-step workflow, when should something be escalated to a human versus handled automatically.

That's what AI unlocks. Not just the plumbing, but the judgment. Kryon's agents don't just trigger actions. They reason through workflows. They handle the edge cases. They know when something doesn't look right and flag it for review. They complete jobs end to end, across every tool in your stack, without needing someone to watch over them.

And the whole thing is designed so that a smart operations person — not a developer — can build, deploy, and manage it themselves. In minutes, not months.

What I believe

I believe that the companies that will win the next decade aren't necessarily the ones with the most people or the most capital. They're the ones that figure out how to operate with the highest leverage — where every person on the team is spending their time on the things that actually matter, and the rest is handled.

I believe that the technology to make that happen is here right now. The only thing standing between most businesses and that future is the tooling — tools that are simple enough for any team to use, powerful enough to handle real workflows, and reliable enough to trust with critical operations.

That's what Kryon is. Not a toy. Not a developer platform. A serious operations tool built for serious businesses, that doesn't require you to have an engineering team to use it.

Where we're going

We're just getting started. The businesses using Kryon today are running hundreds of workflows — sales pipelines, finance operations, customer support queues, onboarding sequences — all on autopilot. The results they're seeing are real: hours reclaimed every week, processes that used to take days now completing in minutes, teams that can finally focus on the work that moves the needle.

We're going to keep building. More integrations, more capable agents, deeper analytics, better tools for teams managing complex operations at scale. But the mission won't change: make powerful automation accessible to every business, not just the ones that can afford to build it from scratch.

If that resonates with you — if you're tired of your team spending their best hours on work that shouldn't require any of them — I'd love to show you what's possible.
